Good Judgment Comes From Experience

And Experience? Well That Comes From Poor Judgment

Posted by Ashlyn Bell IS 201 on April 16th, 2026

Triathalons

Triathlons taught me that there are, in fact, three distinct ways to question your life choices in a single morning. First, you wake up at an hour normally reserved for bakers and regret everything. Then you swim in a crowded lake where being kicked in the face is considered part of the experience. By the time you’re on the bike, you’ve convinced yourself this isn’t so bad—until the run, when your legs transform into overcooked spaghetti and your inner voice becomes a mix of motivational coach and dramatic toddler. Crossing the finish line feels amazing, mostly because it means you can finally sit down without guilt.

Papa Murphy's Pizza

Working at Papa Murphy’s Pizza was my introduction to the fine art of assembling food that you don’t actually cook. It’s like being a culinary middleman—“Here’s your pizza, but you still have to believe in yourself enough to finish it.” I became intimately familiar with the emotional spectrum of customers: the overly enthusiastic coupon user, the indecisive topping philosopher, and the person who orders a plain cheese pizza but somehow makes it sound like a bold lifestyle choice. I left with two key skills: the ability to shred cheese at lightning speed and a deep respect for anyone who works in food service without losing their sanity.

Certified Nursing Assistant

Being a CNA (Certified Nursing Assistant) quickly humbles you in ways no other job can. You walk in thinking you’re there to help people, and you absolutely are—but you also become a professional in tasks no one ever brags about at parties. It’s physically exhausting, emotionally intense, and occasionally absurd, like when a patient insists you’re secretly their long-lost cousin. But it also teaches patience, compassion, and how to function on approximately four hours of sleep and sheer determination. It’s the kind of work that sticks with you, mostly because it changes how you see people—and because your back will never quite forgive you.

Missionary

Serving as an LDS missionary is like signing up for an eighteen month crash course in rejection, resilience, and learning how to start conversations with complete strangers without sounding like a robot. You knock on doors, smile a lot, and develop an uncanny ability to pivot when someone says, “Not interested,” before you’ve even finished saying hello. There are moments of real connection that make it all worthwhile, along with plenty of moments where you’re just trying to figure out how you ended up discussing eternal truths while standing on someone’s porch in the freezing cold. You come home with stories, perspective, and a newfound appreciation for normal conversation pacing.

Broke College Student

Living as a poor college student is less of a phase and more of a creative survival experiment. You learn that “meal planning” really means figuring out how many different ways you can justify eating instant noodles in a week. Your bank account balance becomes a source of both anxiety and dark humor, and you develop a sixth sense for spotting free food on campus. At the same time, there’s something oddly enjoyable about the chaos—late-night study sessions, questionable financial decisions, and the shared understanding among friends that nobody actually knows what they’re doing. It’s a little stressful, a little ridiculous, and somehow still a good time.
